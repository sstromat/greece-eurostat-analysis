01 ⚙️

Productive Capacity

PARTIAL IMPROVEMENT Real GDP recovered strongly from its 2020 trough and surpassed the 2019 baseline by 2022, with investment (GFCF) recovering in parallel — partly EU-fund driven. However labour productivity per hour worked is essentially flat against 2019, meaning growth is being generated through more hours worked rather than higher output per worker. Unit labour costs declined, aiding competitiveness but partly reflecting wage compression. The sectoral structure is unchanged: tourism and trade still dominate; industry remains marginal. Cyclical GDP recovery without productive transformation.

02 👷

Labour Allocation

IMPROVED Unemployment fell sharply from ~17% in 2019 to below 9% by 2024 — its lowest in over a decade. Youth unemployment declined substantially though remains above 20%. Broader labour slack (including discouraged workers) narrowed in parallel. Job quality improved: involuntary part-time and temporary shares both declined post-2021. The minimum wage rose from €758 to over €968/month in nominal terms. Self-employment remains elevated — a known proxy for precarious or informal work arrangements. Genuine labour market improvement; quality gains real but partial.

03 🏠

Household Resilience

MIXED Poverty and material deprivation declined from 2019 levels but remain among the EU's highest. The Gini coefficient improved marginally. Housing stress is the key deterioration: the cost overburden rate worsened post-2021 as rents accelerated faster than incomes. The 2022 energy price shock severely compressed real purchasing power; consumer confidence recovered from COVID lows but remains below pre-crisis norms. Household saving rates rose during COVID then compressed during the inflation regime. Slow improvement on poverty; clear deterioration on housing affordability.

04 🏛️

Fiscal Sustainability

STRONGLY IMPROVED Greece's fiscal position is the clearest improvement since 2019. The general government balance moved from deep pandemic deficits to primary and then overall surplus by 2024. Public debt declined from its peak above 200% of GDP toward ~154% — still the EU's highest but on a clear downward path. Interest payments as a share of GDP declined — a direct benefit of debt restructuring terms. Revenue held up; expenditure consolidated while maintaining social protection levels. Risk: fiscal improvement is partially EU-fund driven — growth boosts revenues while transfers substitute for domestic investment. Genuine consolidation; debt level and EU-fund dependency remain structural constraints.

05 ✈️

External Viability

DETERIORATED The external position is the clearest deterioration since 2019. Despite record tourism receipts, the current account balance worsened materially — driven by a structurally widening goods deficit and growing net income outflows. Service exports reached high levels but were insufficient to offset import growth. The net international investment position remains deeply negative, reflecting accumulated external liabilities. FDI inflows are volatile and concentrated in real estate and tourism — not productive manufacturing or technology. Export volumes grew but from a narrow, unchanged sectoral base. External accounts deteriorated despite strong tourism — structural goods deficit unresolved.

Financial Intermediation

PARTIALLY IMPROVED — data limited Note: Eurostat does not publish NPL ratios or bank interest rate data — those are ECB/SSM sources. From ECB data (referenced not charted): NPL ratios fell from ~49% peak to ~3.3% by 2024 — a dramatic clean-up. The data here covers sectoral financial accounts (balance sheets). Household financial liabilities declined relative to GDP — consistent with ongoing deleveraging. Corporate balance sheets reflect continuing restructuring. Banking sector total assets contracted as a share of GDP — confirming the known loan-to-GDP decline from ~130% to ~65%. The system is measurably safer, but credit to the real economy remains constrained. Balance sheet clean-up confirmed; credit intermediation recovery still incomplete.

07 👥

Demographic Trajectory

DETERIORATED The demographic picture is the most structurally concerning surface. Population declined continuously from 10.8M in 2015 to 10.4M by 2025. Fertility dropped further to ~1.24 — well below EU average and far below replacement level. Deaths consistently outnumber births. The old-age dependency ratio rose steadily, increasing pension and healthcare pressure. Brain drain of the 25–39 cohort eased since the 2015–16 peak but remains substantial. Life expectancy held up. No policy since 2019 has materially altered this trajectory. All demographic indicators deteriorating — the most unmanaged structural risk.

08 ⚡

Energy & Climate

IMPROVED Renewable share in electricity generation surged from ~35% in 2019 to over 50% by 2023-24, driven overwhelmingly by solar. GHG emissions declined steadily, with the energy sector leading reductions. Energy intensity of the economy improved. The 2022 gas price spike was severe — exposing continued vulnerability to gas market volatility despite the renewable build-out. Prices partially normalised by 2023-24. The transition is real but incomplete: gas remains a significant balancing fuel. Genuine decarbonisation progress; gas price vulnerability remains unresolved.

09 💡

Modernisation & Innovation

MIXED — surface gains, structural weakness Digital infrastructure improved: internet penetration and broadband quality rose; e-government use expanded — partly forced by COVID. Cloud adoption and e-commerce grew. However the innovation base remains thin: R&D as % of GDP is among the EU's lowest and showed no meaningful increase despite RRF digital pillar funding. Patents per capita remain very low. AI adoption is nascent. High-tech employment share barely moved. The ICT sector's share of GVA is small and flat. Adoption of digital tools improved; genuine innovation capacity did not.

10 🏥

Health System

MIXED — ICU capacity improved, access barriers persisting The Greek health system suffered severe capacity cuts during the 2010–2018 austerity decade. Since 2019, public hospital bed numbers stabilised and ICU capacity expanded significantly post-COVID (+16% 2019→2023). Unmet medical needs due to cost declined from a crisis peak of 10.9% (2015) to 5.7% (2021), but then rose sharply back to 9.4% (2023) — a reversal driven by the inflation shock eroding household budgets. Out-of-pocket expenditure as a share of total health spending remains high, reflecting weak public system coverage. Preventable mortality spiked during COVID but recovered to below 2019 levels by 2023. Treatable mortality improved steadily. ICU expansion is genuine progress; the cost-driven access barrier reversed after 2021 — a direct inflation-era impact on health equity.

11 🎓

Education & Human Capital

SLOWLY IMPROVING Early school leaving declined from 2019 levels, continuing a long-term trend, though Greece still exceeds the EU average. Tertiary attainment among 25-34 year-olds improved steadily — but this partly reflects emigration of less-educated cohorts rather than purely domestic attainment gains. Public education expenditure is modest and declined in real terms during austerity, with limited recovery since 2019. The unemployment premium for low-education workers is extremely high in Greece — well above EU norms — indicating the labour market strongly penalises low attainment. Adult participation in learning remains low. Structural improvement in attainment; expenditure and adult learning remain weak.

12 🏗️

Housing Market

DETERIORATED Greece lacks a house price index in Eurostat (data not submitted), but all proxy indicators point to deterioration. Housing cost overburden — % of income spent on housing above 40% — worsened post-2021 as rents surged well above income growth. Severe housing deprivation for market renters is notably higher than for owners with a mortgage, reflecting the vulnerability of the rental segment. Homeownership rates, while high by EU standards, declined slightly as younger cohorts are being priced out. Building permits recovered from their near-zero austerity lows, signalling some supply response — but insufficient relative to demand in Athens and tourist-driven island markets. Housing affordability is a genuine and worsening crisis — policy response has been absent.

13 🗺️

Regional Convergence

MIXED — aggregate improving, regional gaps persistent At the national level, regional statistics show aggregate improvement in unemployment across all Greek NUTS2 regions since 2019. However, the economic geography of Greece remains highly polarised: Attica (Athens) dominates GDP and household income, accounting for roughly 40% of national economic activity. Peripheral regions — Western Macedonia, Epirus, Eastern Macedonia & Thrace — show persistently lower incomes, higher unemployment, and continued population loss. The regional dispersion of unemployment, while narrowing, remains wide. EU cohesion fund flows are large but their impact on structural convergence is limited — Athens absorbs a disproportionate share. Macro improvement masks persistent and structural regional divergence.

14 🧵

Social Fabric

MIXED — opportunity improved, safety and inclusion mixed The safety picture is more complex than aggregate crime headlines suggest: robbery and drug offences declined since 2019, but theft (2.8→10.1 per 100k), assault (1.1→1.8), and burglary (0.6→6.6) all increased substantially — in part reflecting post-COVID catch-up and changed reporting patterns. Life satisfaction improved modestly (5.7 in 2013 to 6.4 by 2025) but remains among EU's lower scores. The NEET rate fell from 17.7% (2019) to 14.2% (2024). Childcare for under-3s improved strongly from 14% to 20% — a positive structural shift. However, the gender pay gap worsened (10.4%→13.4%) and the disability employment gap deteriorated sharply (29%→35.9% by 2025), signalling widening inclusion failures. Opportunity improved; safety is mixed; gender and disability inclusion deteriorated.

15 ⚖️

Governance & Institutions

DETERIORATED — CPI flat, judicial trust collapsed Greece's Corruption Perceptions Index (TI, embedded in Eurostat SDG framework) moved from 48 in 2019 to 50 in 2025 — essentially flat, with significant year-to-year volatility. Perceived judicial independence deteriorated sharply: those rating it as good or very good fell from 57% (2019) to just 38% (2025), while those rating it bad or very bad rose from 34% to 60%. This is one of the starkest negative trends in the dataset. Civil court cases pending per 100k increased from 2,877 to 3,173 between 2019 and 2022 — a worsening backlog. Civil servant headcount stabilised after deep austerity cuts; remuneration partially recovered but remains depressed. Judicial trust collapsed post-2021; corruption perception flat; court backlog growing — institutional deterioration is the key finding.

Regime-Conditional Analysis

2019 — BASELINE

Pre-shock: high unemployment (~17%), large debt (~180% GDP), low productivity, weak external balance, demographic decline already entrenched. Structurally fragile starting point.

2020–21 — COVID

GDP collapsed then rebounded. Fiscal deficits widened but managed. Labour market cushioned by transfers. Tourism crushed then surged. Not directly comparable to steady state.

2022–23 — INFLATION

Real wages compressed sharply. Housing costs accelerated. Tourism boomed. Fiscal tightened faster than expected. Energy crisis exposed gas dependency. Nominal GDP gains partly illusory.

2024–26 — NORMALISATION

The test period. Labour market holding. Fiscal surplus sustained. Inflation moderating. But: productivity still flat, current account still deteriorating, demographics worsening, housing unresolved. Structural position unchanged.

Final Verdict: Stabilisation Without Transformation

Greece in 2025 is unambiguously more stable than in 2019: lower unemployment, fiscal surplus, cleaner banking system, higher renewable share, lower GHG emissions, declining crime. These are real improvements and should not be dismissed. 3 of 15 surfaces improved; 8 are mixed; 4 deteriorated.

How to characterise Greece as a whole: A post-crisis economy in managed stabilisation — fiscally disciplined, externally fragile, socially stressed, and institutionally weakening. It functions as a tourism-and-transfers state: domestic productive capacity is insufficient to sustain growth without recurring injections of external demand (tourists) and external capital (EU funds). The state apparatus improved its financial management while losing public trust. Society is better employed but worse housed and more unequal in key dimensions. The long-run trajectory — demographic collapse, brain drain, flat productivity, deteriorating institutions — points toward a slow structural decline if the current growth model is not actively replaced. Greece is not in crisis; it is in a prolonged low-equilibrium trap that looks stable from the outside but is quietly eroding from within.

However the structural growth model is identical to 2019: tourism-dominated, low-productivity, low-innovation, demographically challenged, externally vulnerable, regionally polarised, and institutionally weak. The improvements are largely cyclical (post-pandemic rebound), exogenous (EU fund injection equivalent to ~20% of GDP), or sectoral (energy transition driven by economics as much as policy). Where EU funds went — digital, green, innovation — the output indicators (patents, R&D intensity, productivity) show no corresponding uplift.

The three unmanaged structural risks — demographic collapse, housing unaffordability, and regional divergence — are interconnected and reinforcing. Young people cannot afford housing, delay or forgo families, and emigrate. Brain drain removes the human capital needed to upgrade the economic model. Regional peripheries depopulate while Athens absorbs disproportionate activity and EU resources. No policy framework since 2019 has addressed this nexus.

Greece improved since 2019 primarily because of external capital injection (EU funds ~€36B) and a global tourism supercycle — not because of internal structural change. Remove those tailwinds and the baseline fragilities remain fully intact.
